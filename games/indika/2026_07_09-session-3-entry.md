## Sequence

Vertical elevator/box combo → dense 19th-century living quarters → guard tableau →
scaffolding + steam machine → corridor → the gravity room (reunion with Ilya) → corridor
to Spasov cathedral quarter → bell courtyard → scaffolding follow-section → crane + Lego-brick
bridge minigame → church conflict → flashback as Miro (Pac-Man chase).

## Observations

1. **Familiar toolkit, no new teaching.** A vertically split level reuses everything taught
   so far — elevator, platforms set at Indika's arm height, and a movable box — combined into
   one small puzzle at the top. No new mechanic, just recombination of the existing ones; light
   still does the final guiding to the exit.
2. **A city too detailed to need NPCs.** Entering the dense 19th-century living quarters,
   the commentary singles this out as a deliberately slow section built purely to showcase level
   art. Almost no NPCs populate it, yet the debris, signage, and layout alone sell it as a place
   people actually live and use — detail carrying inhabitedness without needing bodies in it.
3. **Two bridges, one door.** Two visible bridges, only one enters — the other is a look-but-
   can't-touch landmark, the same illusory-choice/fake-corridor logic flagged in session 2 (**NP-02**):
   the world shows you an alternate route it never lets you take.
4. **Heavy safe as box-variant.** The familiar "movable box" mechanic reappears reskinned as a
   heavy safe — same interaction, new dressing, used to reach a higher opening.
5. **A guard who doesn't care.** A staged, eerie beat: a police/safeguard dancing to music from
   his own player, entirely ignoring Indika as she passes through what reads as a prison section.
   Pure atmosphere — no interaction offered or expected.
6. **Light-through-window exit.** Once again, a single lit window is the only signal needed to
   find the way out — the game trusts this cue completely by now, no other markers required.
7. **Scaffolding + steam machine, short and unhard.** A wooden-plank scaffolding traversal is
   interrupted by a broken platform; solved with a short, low-difficulty mini-game positioning an
   extra platform from a nearby elevator. Explicitly called out as "not demanding" — a beat of
   relief/spectacle (a beautiful steam machine) rather than a real test.
8. **Forced corridor, no branching.** A cutscene hands off into a strict corridor section through
   a part of the city tied to Indika's father/past — no choice of direction at all.
9. **The gravity room — new mechanic, thematic peak.** A boiler-room/industrial space where Ilya
   stands in a corner under *different gravity* than Indika. Light marks his spot as before, but the
   puzzle is new: move a single box, then walk the room until your own "down" matches his. Two of the
   room's three exits look back into the *same room repeating itself* at a different gravity
   orientation — Portal-like recursive framing — and through them Indika sees a parallel version of
   herself: the devil, in physical form, for the first time. The puzzle itself is called easy; its
   weight is entirely thematic, not mechanical.
10. **Bells bigger than the church.** Following Ilya through a new, wealthier quarter toward a
    cathedral, the giant bells hanging over the courtyard continue the game's running scale-shift motif
    (previously: the giant fish) — architecture built specifically to make the world feel surreally
    oversized around the characters.
11. **A pure-observation corridor.** A scaffolding climb with literally no choices and — notably —
    no dialogue either; called out as existing purely so the player can look around and orient, not to
    be tested or told anything.
12. **Elevator-call + crane/Lego-brick minigame, reprised.** The elevator-calling mechanic (send
    Ilya up/down) returns, followed by a second crane-operation minigame — this time placing
    Lego-brick-like arches and bridge segments rather than session 2's Tetris pieces. Same teach-once,
    vary-later logic as the counterweight elevator; same giant-machine/tiny-player scale contrast.
13. **Conflict, then a different flashback — playing Miro.** A church confrontation triggers a
    flashback in the familiar pixelated 8-bit style, but for the first time it isn't a
    platformer and isn't played as Indika: it's a Pac-Man-style chase as Miro, her first lover,
    stealing money from Indika's father to fund their escape, dodging thrown wrenches while
    collecting all the gold before the sequence ends.

### New threads from the recording (beyond sessions 1–2)

- **The devil made physical** — the mirrored-gravity room is the first time the devil appears in a
  visible, embodied form rather than just as a voice, doing so through the level geometry itself
  (two exits looking into a parallel, differently-oriented copy of the same room). Strong candidate
  for its own narrative-design writeup alongside **NP-02**.
- **A gravity-orientation puzzle, unannounced.** New spatial mechanic (align your "down" with a
  target's) introduced and resolved within a single room, never taught elsewhere — a self-contained
  puzzle built to carry theme rather than to be reused.
- **Flashback genre keeps shifting, not just visual style.** Session 1–2 established a *pixel-art
  platformer* as "memory's genre." This session swaps the genre itself — a Pac-Man-style chase — while
  keeping the pixelated dressing constant, and swaps protagonist too (Miro, not Indika). Memory as a
  different game *and* a different playable character.
- **Teach-once-vary-later, again.** The elevator-call and crane minigames both return with new
  skins (Lego bricks instead of Tetris pieces) — reinforcing the pattern already logged in session 2.
- **Scale shift as a running motif, confirmed a third time.** Giant fish → giant crane machinery →
  now giant bells over a church. Consistently used to make the world feel oversized/surreal around
  the small player character.
- **Silence as a guidance tool.** The no-choice, no-dialogue scaffolding climb is a new variant of
  pacing control — pure environment, no narrative scaffolding at all, unlike the usual corridor
  sections which are typically paired with conversation.
